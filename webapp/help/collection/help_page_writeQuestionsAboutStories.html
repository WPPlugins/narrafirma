Write questions about stories

Here is where you can write questions to ask people about their stories. You'll draw from these questions when you create your story forms.

What to do here

Add some questions to your list. For each question, write what you want to ask people. Choose what type of question it is (see below for an explanation of the types). Also give each question a short name (so there's room to write the question name in tables and on graphs).

You can copy questions from built-in templates (suggestions). Change the blank spaces (_____) in the question to suit your project and participants.

For help choosing, click the "Recommendations for story questions" button. You'll see a table that shows the various options, color-coded with evaluations of risk, connected to the answers you gave to questions about your participant groups and resources.

Frequently-asked questions
Why ask questions about stories?

Asking questions helps people make sense of their own stories. These interpretations increase your project's breadth and depth of exploration, leading to deeper insights. When you ask not just "what happened" but "what does that mean to you," you've gone further and deeper in.

How many questions should I ask about each story?
  • For a kiosk type project, in which people are just walking by and have no commitment to the project at all, you can ask three to five questions about each story.
  • For a volunteer type project, in which people feel socially obligated to participate, you can ask six to nine questions about each story.
  • For a commitment type project, in which people feel that participating is linked to their identities, you can ask ten to fifteen questions about each story.
How do I set the order of the questions?

You don't set the question order here. You only create the questions here. You set the order of questions when you design your story form. That's so you can reuse your questions in multiple story forms.

What do the question types mean?

A boolean question shows the options "yes" and "no," of which people can choose only one.

A label is just some plain text. It's not really a question. Nothing gets stored. It's a way to talk to people between your questions.

A header is the same as a label, only it's formatted as bold text.

A checkbox question has one solitary check box. It starts out unchecked. To add a label to the checkbox (e.g., "Yes, I think so"), enter it into the choices box.

A checkboxes question has any number of check box answers, and people can tick as many as they like. Enter labels for your check boxes in the choices box. (There is no way to limit people to a fixed number of answers. Use your question text to ask them to limit their choices, for example "Please choose up to three answers.")

A text question has a one-line text box, for short but free-text answers.

A textarea question has a multi-line, extendible text box, for paragraph-length free-text answers.

A select question has a single-choice drop-down list. Enter your list items in the choices box.

A radiobuttons question has any number of answers, from which people can choose only one. These are like buttons on a radio: when you click one choice, all the others "pop up." Enter the answers you want to make available in the choices box.

A slider question has a line with a button that you drag. Labels on the left and right show people what they are choosing between. In the choices box, enter first the left-side slider label, then (on the second line), the right-side slider label. If you want special text for the "Does not apply" checkbox under the slider (meaning, I don't want to set a value), write it in the third line of the choices box. For example, you might write:

  • a little
  • a lot
  • I don't know

If you don't specify a third-line label, the checkbox will say "Does not apply."

What's the difference between a "boolean" and a "check box"?

A check box has only two possible answers: yes and no. It starts out unchecked, and you can't tell whether people meant to say "no" or didn't touch it. A boolean question, in contrast, has three possible answers: yes, no, and unanswered. It starts out with neither radio button clicked, and you can tell whether people clicked "no" or didn't click anything.

Can I use HTML formatting for questions?

You can use these HTML tags: address, article, b, big, blockquote, br, caption, cite, code, del, div, dd, d1, dt, em, h1, h2, h3, h4, h5, h6, hr, i, kbd, li, ol, p, pre, s, small, span, sup, sub, strong, strike, table, td, th, tr, u, ul.

You can also use a smaller subset of HTML tags (b, big, em, i, s, small, sup, sub, strong, strike, u) in your specified answers (choices) for checkboxes and radio buttons.

HTML tags affect how your question texts appear in your online survey and in the "Enter stories" screen. They are stripped out when you print your story form.

To protect your NarraFirma installation from hacking, you cannot use attributes (such as "id") on any of these tags.

Can I create my own templates?

Only if you are comfortable editing the source code files in your installation of NarraFirma. If you are, find the file called "templates.js" and add your template to it. (Backup the file first!) Follow the formatting in the templates you see, and make sure you put your template in the right part of the file. If NarraFirma won't start up after you make your changes, check your browser's console to find out where the error lies.
